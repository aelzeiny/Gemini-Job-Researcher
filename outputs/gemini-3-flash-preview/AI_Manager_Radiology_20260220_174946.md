# AI Manager (Radiology)

*Generated: 2026-02-20 17:49:46*

---

The role of an **AI Manager in Radiology** is a specialized, high-level position that sits at the intersection of clinical medicine, data science, and IT operations. Unlike clinical roles, this is primarily a leadership and administrative position focused on the implementation, validation, and maintenance of artificial intelligence tools (such as automated triage, image segmenting, and diagnostic assistance).

---

## 1. Cognitive Demands
*   **Type of Thinking:** This role requires **systems thinking** and **high-level analytical reasoning**. You must understand how an algorithm affects the entire clinical workflow. It requires "detective-like" thinking to troubleshoot why an AI tool might be underperforming (e.g., "algorithmic drift").
*   **Math & Quantitative Reasoning:** **Strong.** While you may not be coding algorithms from scratch, you must deeply understand statistics (sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, AUC-ROC curves) to evaluate whether a tool is safe for patient use. You also need quantitative skills for ROI (Return on Investment) calculations to justify costs to hospital boards.
*   **Mental Stamina:** High. A typical day involves shifting between technical meetings with engineers, clinical meetings with radiologists, and financial meetings with executives. This context-switching is mentally taxing.

## 2. Burnout Risk & Job Pressure
*   **Burnout Risk Level:** **Moderate.** It lacks the "compassion fatigue" of nursing but suffers from "bureaucratic friction."
*   **Primary Stressors:** 
    *   **Implementation Resistance:** Dealing with radiologists who fear AI will replace them or who find the tools interrupt their workflow.
    *   **Vendor Management:** Constant pressure from AI startups selling "snake oil" versus finding evidence-based solutions.
    *   **Technical Failures:** If an AI triage tool for strokes goes down, it creates an immediate clinical crisis.
*   **Legal Liability:** High-stakes but indirect. While the radiologist signs the report, the AI Manager is responsible for the **governance framework**. If a system is improperly calibrated and leads to mass misdiagnosis, the AI Manager faces significant professional and legal scrutiny regarding their validation protocols.
*   **Autonomy vs. Oversight:** Generally high autonomy. You are often the "subject matter expert," but you operate under the oversight of the Chief Information Officer (CIO) or Chief of Radiology.

## 3. Emotional Intensity
*   **Level of Emotional Labor:** **Low to Moderate.** The labor is not "patient-facing"; it is "colleague-facing." You are managing the anxieties and expectations of medical staff.
*   **Trauma/End-of-Life:** **None.** You are removed from the bedside. You deal with pixels and data, not blood and grief.
*   **Patient Interaction:** Effectively zero.
*   **Coping Strategies:** The most important strategy is **political navigation.** You must be able to handle "ego management" when dealing with high-level clinicians and stay patient with the slow pace of hospital digital transformation.

## 4. Physical Demands
*   **Physical Toll:** **Sedentary.** This is an office-based or remote-friendly role.
*   **Repetitive Strain:** High risk of "tech worker" issues: carpal tunnel syndrome, eye strain (from monitors), and back/neck pain from prolonged sitting.
*   **Heavy Lifting:** None.
*   **Physical Issues:** Weight gain and postural issues due to a lack of movement during the shift.

## 5. Schedule & Hours
*   **Typical Shifts:** Standard **Business Hours (9 AM – 5 PM, Mon-Fri)**.
*   **Nights/Weekends/Holidays:** Very rare. You might work an evening or weekend during a major software "go-live" or system upgrade, but this is the exception, not the rule.
*   **Predictability:** **High.** Unlike a radiologist who may have a chaotic reading room, your schedule is mostly dictated by planned meetings and project timelines.
*   **Seniority:** Does not typically dictate shifts, as everyone works standard business hours.

## 6. Compensation
*   **Typical Salary Range (USA):** **$125,000 – $190,000+** per year. 
    *   *Entry-level (within the specialty):* $110k–$130k.
    *   *Experienced/Director Level:* $180k–$230k (especially in large academic systems or private groups).
*   **Variations:** Salary is significantly higher in tech hubs (San Francisco, Boston, NYC) and within large private "Super-groups" compared to small community hospitals.
*   **Benefits:** Standard executive-level benefits: Comprehensive health/dental, 401(k) with high matching, and 3–5 weeks of PTO.
*   **Bonuses:** Annual performance bonuses (5–15%) are common in this role, often tied to meeting implementation milestones or efficiency targets.

## 7. Work-Life Balance
*   **Overall Impact:** **Excellent**, especially compared to almost any other role in a hospital. 
*   **On-Call:** Rare. You might be "on-call" for technical emergencies, but these are usually handled by the IT help desk first.
*   **Personal Life:** This role is highly sustainable. It allows for consistent exercise, family dinners, and hobbies. 
*   **Sustainability:** Practitioners generally view this as a long-term career. It is a "future-proof" role; as AI becomes more integrated into healthcare, the demand for people who can manage it will only grow. The main threat to sustainability is "meeting fatigue" and the slow-moving nature of healthcare administration.

### Summary Honest Take
**The Reward:** You are at the cutting edge of medicine. You get to influence how thousands of patients are diagnosed without the physical and emotional exhaustion of bedside care. The pay is high, and the hours are "normal."

**The Challenge:** You will spend 70% of your time in meetings trying to convince people to change. You will face significant "red tape" and must have the patience to navigate complex hospital politics and cybersecurity regulations. If you hate spreadsheets and "corporate speak," this role will be frustrating.
